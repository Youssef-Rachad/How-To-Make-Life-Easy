Is there such a thing as having too much time?

    • 1. DEVELOPPING THE WEBSITE HAS TO BE EASY


      No point in complicating it. The website is here to assist your time management, hence the name of the blog.

    • 2. IT NEEDS TO BOOST YOUR PRODUCTIVITY

    • 3. IT MUST REWARD YOU FOR THE EFFORTS INVESTED

    I would like to talk more in-depth about the general idea of starting a habit.

    To start off, making a website is about building the habit of recording and planning your workload so as to stay on top of it.

    To better understand how habits influence the activities we commit to, I turned to James Clear’s Atomic Habits. As mentioned in the Introduction blog, this project is not a one-time little trial, thus reading Atomic Habits enlightened me on long term strategies. I would like to share with you my two key takeaways from this wonderful expert book.

    1. The key to success is in finding what is right for you.
    James Clear writes:

    “Many people think they lack motivation when what they really lack is clarity” (Clear, 70)

    and suggests what researchers call an implementation intention. I extend its definition to include a “what task will I do?” clause such that the final intention goes along the lines of: “ I will [design/create/test/] my [webpage] after [a specific time]”. The specific time refers to a moment in your day that would be convenient, such as after checking emails at your computer, or before starting your favourite class at 2:30 pm.

    2. Simplify the technical management side as much as you can.
    In his book, James Clear sets down 4 laws that motivate you to keep your habit going. The 3rd such law dictates that a habit is prone to survive if it is made easy to practice. Also, Clear backs his law with the physics Principle of Least Action which stipulates:

    “The path taken by the system between times t1 and t2 and configurations q1 and q2 is the one for which the action is stationary (no change) to first order” (Penrose 474).


    I find this to be a physicist's way of saying: “the best way to maintain a website is to keep its access straightforward” and, just as in any physics problem, friction will keep you from achieving. This means keeping your main folder on your desktop screen, labeling your sub-folders logically and maintaining a good ratio of functionality to readability in your code.
    To put all of this more intuitively, consider the following question: “what probably scares most people in creating their own website?”. My guess is that a common misconception leads to believing websites to be these complexe monstrosities, though dealing with a complex entity is very daunting. Therefore, As mentioned in the foundation above, there’s no point in mixing around your files and writing convoluted code.
